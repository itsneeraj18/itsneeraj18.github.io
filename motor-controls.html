Motor Control Fundamentals

How motors are commanded, limited, and shaped to meet real application demands.

Motor control is not about equations first — it is about deciding what behavior the machine must produce. This page builds intuition from application needs and only then connects them to control strategies.

1. What Is Motor Control?

Every motor control problem begins with a simple decision.

Do we need to control torque, or do we need to control speed?

Control systems exist to enforce mechanical behavior at the shaft. Mathematics is a tool — not the goal.

2. Torque vs Speed — Application View

  • EV traction: Torque commanded directly, speed emerges
  • Conveyors & cranes: Torque dominates under load changes
  • CNC spindles: Speed must remain constant
  • Fans & pumps: Speed sets flow

Most dynamic systems are torque-controlled. Speed control is usually layered on top.

3. Motor and Generator Are the Same Machine

  • Mechanical rotation → voltage appears
  • Applied voltage exceeds EMF → motoring
  • EMF exceeds applied voltage → generating

Regeneration is not a special mode. It is the same machine obeying the same laws.

4. Back-EMF in PMSM Machines

Permanent magnets induce voltage in the stator as the rotor spins. This voltage — back-EMF — defines both speed limits and regeneration.

Back-EMF video demonstration

Back-EMF magnitude scales with speed and flux: E ∝ ω × Φ

5. What Can Actually Be Controlled?

  1. Flux (field strength)
  2. Torque-producing current

Speed is never controlled directly. It is the outcome of torque balance.

6. Torque–Speed Envelope

Every operating point lies at the intersection of:

  • Motor capability
  • Load demand
  • Controller limits

Control strategies exist to stay inside this envelope safely.

7. Where Control Strategies Fit

  • Open-loop V/f: Bring-up and light load
  • FOC: Precise torque control
  • Advanced: MTPA, flux weakening, observers
