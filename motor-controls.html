Motor Control Fundamentals

Understanding what we control, why we control it, and how control strategies emerge from application requirements.

1. What Is Motor Control?

Before discussing algorithms, transformations, or PWM techniques, motor control starts with a simple but critical question:

What does the application want from the motor at this moment ‚Äî torque or speed?

This decision defines the entire control strategy. Whether you are working on electric vehicles, industrial drives, robotics, or HVAC systems, motor control exists to enforce a desired mechanical behavior at the shaft.

Speed and torque are not independent choices ‚Äî they are linked by the mechanical load. Control systems decide which variable must be regulated tightly and which can be a consequence of physics.

2. Torque vs Speed ‚Äî Application Perspective

Most real-world applications can be categorized by whether they primarily demand torque control or speed control.

  • Electric Vehicles: Driver pedal commands torque. Speed is a result of vehicle dynamics and road load.
  • Conveyors & Cranes: Torque control ensures smooth motion under varying load.
  • CNC Spindles: Speed must remain constant despite cutting forces.
  • Fans & Pumps: Speed control dominates for flow and efficiency regulation.

In practice, torque control dominates dynamic systems, while speed control is used when steady-state behavior must be enforced.

This is why EV traction drives are fundamentally torque-controlled systems with speed supervision layered on top.

3. Electric Machines: Motor and Generator Are the Same Device

An electric machine is inherently reversible.

  • Rotate the shaft mechanically ‚Üí voltage appears at the terminals (generator action).
  • Apply voltage greater than back-EMF ‚Üí torque is produced (motor action).
  • Allow back-EMF to dominate ‚Üí energy flows back to the supply (regeneration).

This reversibility is not a special feature ‚Äî it is a direct consequence of electromagnetic laws. Regenerative braking in EVs and energy recovery in industrial systems rely entirely on this principle.

From a control perspective, the inverter‚Äôs job is always the same: apply the correct voltage vector to overcome or cooperate with back-EMF.

4. What Can Actually Be Controlled?

Although applications talk about speed and torque, an AC motor fundamentally allows independent control of only two electromagnetic quantities:

  1. Flux ‚Äî establishes the magnetic field in the machine
  2. Torque-producing current ‚Äî interacts with flux to generate torque

Speed is not directly controlled. It is the mechanical outcome of torque balance between the motor and the load.

Modern control techniques transform three-phase currents into a rotating reference frame so that flux and torque behave like independent DC quantities. This is not new physics ‚Äî it is a structured way of applying well-understood principles.

5. Torque‚ÄìSpeed Characteristics Define Everything

Every motor-driven system operates at the intersection of:

  • The motor‚Äôs torque‚Äìspeed capability
  • The load‚Äôs torque demand
  • The controller‚Äôs ability to enforce limits

Understanding constant torque regions, constant power regions, and field-weakening is essential for EVs, industrial drives, and high-speed machines.

Control strategies exist to ensure the machine follows this envelope safely, efficiently, and repeatably.

6. Where Control Strategies Fit

With the fundamentals in place, different motor control strategies can be understood as tools with specific strengths and limitations:

  • Open-Loop V/f Control: Simple scalar control for motor bring-up and light-load applications.
  • Field-Oriented Control (FOC): Precise torque control across the full speed range ‚Äî dominant in EV traction systems.
  • Advanced Extensions: MTPA, flux weakening, sensorless observers, and efficiency optimization.

Each method exists because a specific application demands it ‚Äî not because the mathematics is elegant.
