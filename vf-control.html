Open-Loop V/f Control of PMSM/BLDC Motors

What this page covers

This page explains open-loop V/f (Volts-per-Hertz) control as used for PMSM and BLDC motors. The focus is on why it works, how it is implemented, and where it fails, from a practical engineering perspective.

By the end of this page, you should be able to:

  • Understand the operating principle of V/f control
  • Implement a basic V/f controller using SPWM
  • Identify startup and low-speed challenges
  • Decide when V/f control is appropriate ‚Äî and when it is not

1. Where Open-Loop V/f Control Fits

Open-loop V/f control sits at the simplest end of the motor control spectrum.

It does not attempt to:

  • control torque explicitly
  • regulate current
  • track rotor position

Instead, it assumes the motor rotor will follow a rotating stator field generated by the inverter.

This makes it attractive for:

  • motor bring-up
  • no-load or light-load operation
  • cost-sensitive applications
  • lab testing and demonstrations

It also makes it fundamentally unsuitable for traction or dynamic applications.

2. Basic Operating Principle

The electrical speed of the motor is set by the stator electrical frequency. The magnetic flux is set by the applied stator voltage.

To maintain approximately constant air-gap flux:

V / f = constant

Increasing frequency increases speed. Increasing voltage maintains flux.

If frequency increases without a corresponding voltage increase, flux weakens and torque drops.

3. Intuitive View (Engineering Perspective)

Think of V/f control as open-loop field generation.

You are:

  • commanding a rotating magnetic field
  • assuming the rotor stays synchronized
  • not correcting for load disturbances

There is no feedback loop to recover if synchronism is lost.

This assumption holds only when:

  • load torque is low
  • acceleration is gentle
  • operating conditions are predictable

5. Startup and Low-Speed Behavior

At standstill:

  • back-EMF is zero
  • torque production is weak
  • rotor position is unknown

As a result, most practical implementations include:

  • a minimum starting frequency
  • voltage boost at low speed
  • a controlled frequency ramp

These measures improve startup reliability but do not guarantee synchronism under load.

6. Flux, Back-EMF, and Load Effects

Motor flux is proportional to V / f.

If V/f is too low ‚Üí weak flux, low torque
If V/f is too high ‚Üí magnetic saturation, heating

Back-EMF increases with speed and opposes the applied voltage. Since back-EMF is not measured in open-loop control, load-dependent speed error accumulates.

Under sudden load changes:

  • current rises uncontrollably
  • rotor may slip
  • synchronism can be lost

This is a fundamental limitation, not an implementation flaw.

7. Torque Control Limitations

In V/f control:

  • flux is assumed, not regulated
  • current is uncontrolled
  • torque becomes load-dependent

Practical consequences:

  • poor dynamic response
  • torque ripple
  • stalling risk under transients

For this reason, V/f control is not a substitute for vector control or FOC.

8. PWM Strategy

Sinusoidal PWM (SPWM) is commonly used.

  • sinusoidal reference vs triangular carrier
  • modulation index defines output voltage
  • linear region operation up to modulation index ‚âà 1

At higher speeds:

  • DC bus voltage limits output
  • voltage margin reduces
  • efficiency drops

9. Practical Implementation Considerations

Open-loop V/f control may look trivial on paper, but real hardware imposes constraints that must be handled deliberately. Most failures seen during bring-up are not due to theory, but due to poor handling of transitions.

Frequency Ramping

The rotor cannot instantaneously follow a step change in electrical frequency. Always apply a controlled frequency ramp to avoid loss of synchronism.

  • Use linear or S-curve ramps
  • Keep acceleration low during startup
  • Ramp frequency first, then voltage follows via V/f

Voltage Boost at Low Speed

At low frequencies, stator resistance dominates and reduces effective air-gap flux. A small voltage boost is often required to produce usable torque.

  • Apply fixed or proportional voltage offset below base frequency
  • Avoid excessive boost to prevent overheating

Start / Stop Handling

Abrupt start or stop commands can desynchronize the rotor.

  • Always ramp down frequency before stopping
  • Avoid zero-frequency dwell with non-zero voltage

Direction Reversal

Instant frequency sign reversal is unsafe in open-loop control.

  • Ramp frequency to zero
  • Allow rotor to settle
  • Ramp up in opposite direction

These measures do not guarantee stability ‚Äî they merely reduce the likelihood of losing synchronism.

10. Common Open-Loop V/f Implementation Methods

Method 1: Direct Sine Calculation (math.h)

The simplest implementation computes sine values in real time using standard math functions.

  • Easy to understand and implement
  • High CPU load on low-cost MCUs
  • Floating-point dependency

Best suited for simulations, high-end controllers, or early bring-up.

Method 2: Sine Lookup Table (LUT)

A precomputed sine table stored in memory is indexed using a phase accumulator.

  • Low computational overhead
  • Deterministic timing
  • Widely used in embedded systems

Table resolution trades memory for waveform quality.

Method 3: Hall-Assisted Open-Loop (Hybrid)

In some systems, Hall sensor transitions are used only to align phase advance or duty-cycle selection while still operating without current control.

  • Improves robustness during startup
  • Reduces risk of reverse rotation
  • Still not closed-loop torque control

This approach sits between pure open-loop and true closed-loop control.

11. Applications and Non-Applications

‚úÖ Appropriate use cases

  • fans and blowers
  • pumps with predictable load
  • motor bring-up and validation
  • educational and lab setups

‚ùå Avoid V/f control for:

  • EV traction drives
  • robotics and servo systems
  • high-efficiency applications
  • dynamic torque control

12. Key Takeaways

  • Open-loop V/f control is simple, robust, and easy to implement
  • It works only under predictable, light-load conditions
  • No feedback means no correction under disturbances
  • Best used as a starting point, not a final solution

Engineering Warning

If open-loop V/f control works only when the load is light and predictable, it is not "working well" ‚Äî it is simply operating within its assumptions. Any apparent robustness disappears the moment those assumptions break.

Engineer's Note

In real EV inverter development, V/f control is often the first firmware loaded to verify power-stage health before enabling current control and FOC.
