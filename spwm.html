Sinusoidal Pulse Width Modulation (SPWM)

Advanced PWM techniques for smooth, efficient motor control

üí° What is SPWM and Why It Matters

SPWM (Sinusoidal Pulse Width Modulation) is a fundamental technique used to generate sinusoidal AC voltages from a DC bus using high-frequency switching.

SPWM waveform: Sine reference vs. triangle carrier

In motor drives, SPWM enables:

  • Smooth torque: Sinusoidal phase currents reduce torque ripple
  • Harmonic reduction: Lower noise and EMI compared to simpler switching schemes
  • Efficiency: Optimized for different modulation indices and operating points
  • Simplicity: Implementable on standard microcontrollers with PWM peripherals

üìö Theory & Fundamentals

How SPWM Works:

A sinusoidal reference waveform is compared against a high-frequency triangular carrier signal. When the sine wave is higher than the triangle, the inverter switch turns ON. When it's lower, the switch turns OFF. The result is a pulse-width modulated signal whose average value (envelope) follows the sinusoidal reference.

Key Parameters:

  • Modulation Index (m): Ratio of sine amplitude to triangle amplitude. Higher m increases output voltage.
  • Carrier Frequency: Switching frequency (typically 10‚Äì20 kHz). Higher frequency = less filtering needed, but more switching losses.
  • Fundamental Frequency: Motor speed command (0‚Äì400 Hz for EVs)

üîß Implementation

Real-World SPWM Implementation:

  • Timer module generates the triangular carrier (PWM base frequency)
  • ADC samples the phase current and feedback signals
  • Microcontroller computes the sinusoidal reference and compares with carrier
  • PWM duty cycles are updated in real-time (typically every 100 ¬µs)
